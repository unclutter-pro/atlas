# Presentations Reference

Slide decks, pitch materials, keynotes. This is where visual storytelling meets projection constraints.

---

## Type Scale

Presentations need dramatic hierarchy. Use Perfect Fourth (1.333) or Perfect Fifth (1.500).

For 18px body with 1.333 ratio: **18, 24, 32, 43, 57px**

Three text sizes per slide maximum. If you need more, the slide is too complex.

### Text Sizing for Distance

People sit far from projected slides. Size accordingly:

- **Titles:** 36-44pt minimum
- **Body text:** 24-28pt minimum
- **Labels and captions:** 18-20pt minimum
- **Never below 18pt** — if text needs to be smaller, it doesn't belong on the slide

High contrast is non-negotiable. Dark text on light, or light text on dark. Medium contrast that works on your laptop disappears under a projector.

**Text over images:** Minimum 4.5:1 contrast ratio (WCAG AA). Use semi-transparent overlay: `rgba(0,0,0,0.5)` for light text on dark images, or place text in a solid-background box.

## Slide Hierarchy

Not every slide serves the same purpose. Design distinct templates for each role:

- **Title slide** — the opening statement. Name, topic, date. One strong visual. Minimal text.
- **Section divider** — marks transitions between major topics. Large type, bold color, no body text. A single word or phrase, centered.
- **Content slide** — the workhorse. Heading + supporting content. Keep structure consistent.
- **Data slide** — charts, tables, key metrics. The visual IS the content — text supports, doesn't compete.
- **Closing slide** — NOT "Thank you." A specific call-to-action with contact info.

---

## The 6-Word Rule

The most common presentation mistake: cramming multiple ideas onto one slide.

- **Slide titles: 6 words or fewer**
- **Body text: 6 lines or fewer, each line 6 words or fewer**
- Each slide communicates ONE point
- The audience reads the slide in 3 seconds. If they can't grasp the point in that time, it's too complex
- If a slide needs more than 6 lines, split it

---

## Color: The 60/30/10 Rule

- **60%** dominant (background/base)
- **30%** secondary (supporting elements)
- **10%** accent (CTAs, highlights, key data)

Define these three colors and apply them rigidly. AI tends to use too many colors at similar saturation, making them compete.

---

## Spacing

- **Minimum 48-64px margin on all sides** (on 1920×1080). Content should never touch the edge.
- **Consistent alignment:** Elements that shift position between slides create visual noise. Establish a grid and lock to it.
- AI often crowds content to margins. Professional slides have generous breathing room.

## Visual Storytelling Arc

A presentation is a narrative, not a document broken into pages.

1. **Hook** — open with tension, a question, or a surprising fact
2. **Context** — establish why this matters
3. **Journey** — walk through the evidence, building momentum
4. **Climax** — the key insight, the big number, the reveal
5. **Resolution** — what this means, what happens next

Design the slides to reflect this arc. Energy should build — not stay flat. Use color intensity, type scale, and image scale to create momentum.

## Slide Layout Variety

Cycle through these patterns — never repeat the same layout more than twice:

- **Full-bleed image** with overlaid text
- **Split** (50/50 or 60/40) — image + text
- **Centered statement** — one powerful sentence, large
- **Data visualization** — chart with minimal annotation
- **Three-column comparison** — side by side
- **Quote** — large text, attribution below

AI repeats the same layout. Humans notice the monotony.

---

## Chart Simplification for Projection

Charts designed for documents fail on screen at distance. Simplify aggressively:

- **Remove gridlines** — they add noise at projection scale
- **Enlarge labels** — axis labels need 18pt+ on slides
- **Reduce data points** — show the trend, not every data point
- **One highlight** — use accent color for the key data point, grey everything else (Von Restorff Effect)
- **Title the insight, not the chart** — "Revenue grew 40% in Q3" beats "Quarterly Revenue 2024"
- **Bar charts over pie charts** — easier to compare at distance
- **Label data directly** instead of using legends

---

## Animation Restraint

Presentation animation has one job: directing attention.

- **Entrance animations only** — fade in or appear. Content arrives, it doesn't leave.
- **No exit animations** — content disappearing is disorienting
- **No between-element choreography** — bullets flying in from different directions is noise
- **Build slides for progressive reveal** — show one point, then the next. This replaces complex animation with simple sequencing.
- **Transitions between slides:** cut or simple fade. Anything more competes with content.

---

## Speaker Notes Structure

Speaker notes are part of the design — they're the presenter's interface.

- **One key message** per slide — the point to communicate, in one sentence
- **Supporting details** — 2-3 talking points or data references
- **Transition phrase** — how to bridge to the next slide
- **Timing cue** — approximate time for this slide

---

## Common Mistakes

- Logo on every slide — once on the title, once on the closing. Not 40 times.
- Reading the slide aloud — if the speaker says what's written, one of them is redundant
- Decorative clip art or stock photos — every image should carry meaning
- Inconsistent alignment — elements shifting between slides
- Too many fonts — one family (bold for titles, regular for body) is enough
- Dense tables — more than 4 columns and 6 rows belongs in a handout, not a slide
- Every slide the same layout — vary deliberately
- Generic gradients and stock imagery
- "Thank you" as the final slide instead of a clear CTA
